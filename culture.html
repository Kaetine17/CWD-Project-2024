Hanami (Cherry Blossom Viewing)

When: Spring (usually March to May)

What: Hanami is the tradition of enjoying the transient beauty of cherry blossoms. People gather in parks to have picnics under the blooming sakura trees, appreciating the delicate flowers and the fleeting nature of life, symbolized by the short-lived blossoms. It's a time for families, friends, and coworkers to bond and celebrate the season.

Tea Ceremony (Chadō or Sadō)

When: Throughout the year

What: The Japanese tea ceremony is a highly ritualized practice of preparing and drinking matcha (powdered green tea). The ceremony emphasizes aesthetics, etiquette, and mindfulness, reflecting the principles of harmony, respect, purity, and tranquility. It is often performed in a specialized room, and the process is as important as the tea itself.

Obon Festival

When: Mid-August (dates may vary by region)

What: Obon is a Buddhist tradition that honors the spirits of deceased ancestors. It is believed that during Obon, the spirits of the dead return to their families. People clean and decorate graves, and many towns hold festive dances (Bon Odori) and light lanterns (tōrō nagashi) to guide the spirits back. Obon is a time for family reunions and paying respect to ancestors.

New Year's Celebrations (Shōgatsu)

When: January 1st-3rd

What: New Year's (Shōgatsu) is one of the most important holidays in Japan. Families typically celebrate by visiting shrines or temples for the first prayer of the year (Hatsumode), enjoying special foods like osechi (a box of traditional New Year's dishes), and spending time together. It is a time for renewal, gratitude, and hoping for a prosperous year ahead.

Setsubun

When: February 3rd or 4th (the day before the beginning of spring in the lunar calendar)

What: Setsubun is the tradition of driving away evil spirits and welcoming good fortune for the new season. The most famous custom is "mamemaki," in which people throw roasted soybeans at a person dressed as an oni (demon) while chanting "Oni wa soto! Fuku wa uchi!" ("Out with the demons! In with good fortune!"). It’s a fun and lively event, especially in homes and shrines.
